3. Clinical Advice and Treatment Options
What are the best treatment options for specific wound types (e.g., venous leg ulcers, burns, Kennedy ulcers)?


Members frequently seek advice on managing complex wounds like venous leg ulcers (VLUs), burns in children, or Kennedy ulcers in palliative care.


Solution: Describe the wound condition and patient details in the group (e.g., "Advice for 90-year-old with breast cancer wound"). Responses include product recommendations (e.g., Inadine, Cutisorb Sorbact) and clinical approaches (e.g., compression therapy, pain management).


Example Advice: For a breast cancer wound, Inadine and Cutisorb Sorbact were suggested over Bactroban; for burns in children, non-adherent dressings like soft silicone and pain management with Panado syrup were advised.


How do I manage pain and trauma in pediatric burn patients during dressing changes?


Emotional and clinical challenges with pediatric burns are discussed, focusing on pain and PTSD.


Solution: Seek advice on pain management and distraction techniques (e.g., "Advice for dressing changes in a 16-month-old with burns"). Suggestions include pre-dressing pain medication, non-adherent dressings, and engaging the child with toys or games.


Example Advice: Administer Panado syrup 30 minutes before dressing changes and use treats post-dressing to build positive associations.


What are the risks of using certain treatments like crushed Flagyl for odor control?


Discussions on treatments like crushed Flagyl for malodor in fungating tumors highlight potential risks.


Solution: Ask for safe alternatives or guidelines (e.g., "Is crushed Flagyl safe for odor control?"). Members caution against crushing due to inhalation risks and suggest alternatives like Metronidazole gel or Prontosan.


Example Advice: Crushed Flagyl is dangerous due to toxicity risks; Metronidazole gel or charcoal dressings are safer for odor control.


4. Medical Aid and Billing Challenges
Why are wound care authorizations being declined by medical aids like Discovery?


Many members express frustration with declined authorizations from Discovery for conditions like contact dermatitis, burns, or VLUs, often citing "not meeting clinical entry criteria."


Solution: Share specific cases in the group to seek advice or collective action (e.g., "Discovery declined VLU treatment citing 12-week rule"). Members suggest involving patients or doctors for motivation letters, and WHASA often steps in to negotiate with medical aids.


Example Update: WHASA scheduled a meeting with Discovery to discuss wound care protocols, with feedback to be provided on the member portal.


How can I bill correctly for multiple wounds or complex cases?


Questions arise about billing codes for multiple wounds or complex cases to avoid under-billing.


Solution: Ask for billing guidance (e.g., "How to bill for bilateral leg ulcers?"). Responses include using modifiers like 0002 from SPNP tariff guides or consulting billing software like Elixir.


Example Advice: Use SPNP tariff guides and modifier 0002 for additional wounds or sites.


What can I do if medical aid funds are depleted or patients cannot pay?


Concerns about non-payment due to depleted funds or cash practice challenges are common.


Solution: Discuss strategies in the group (e.g., "Patients can't pay after auth depletion"). Suggestions include negotiating with medical aids for ex gratia payments or exploring pro bono options for state patients.


Example Concern: Members noted the difficulty of maintaining a cash practice due to treatment frequency and patient preference for medical aid billing.
